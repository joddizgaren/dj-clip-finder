Yes, a login/licensing layer will help, but it will not make the app uncopyable.

That is the part most people lie to themselves about: with desktop software, “prevent copying” is usually impossible if the app and most of the logic run on the user’s machine. What you can do is make copied versions useless enough that casual sharing stops and resale becomes annoying. GitHub also treats private repository contents as confidential, and releases are the normal way to distribute packaged binaries, but that only controls access to your source/release files, not what a tester can do after downloading the app.  ￼

Your idea is basically the right direction, but you need to understand the limit:
	•	Login/user accounts = access control.
	•	License checks = distribution control.
	•	Server-side features = real leverage.
	•	Obfuscation/signing = friction, not protection.

A copied installer can still be copied. What stops reuse is requiring the app to authenticate against your server before it works, and tying access to an enabled account or license. That model is common in software activation systems. Microsoft’s own licensing docs are an example of activation being used to control use across installations, and GitHub’s private repo model only protects the repo/release access itself.  ￼

What will actually work

For a small private beta with friends, the best setup is:

1. Give each tester their own account

Do not share one test login.

Each tester should have:
	•	unique email/login
	•	password or magic link
	•	account status: active / disabled
	•	optional expiry date
	•	optional max devices: 1 or 2

That way, if your friend sends the installer to someone else, the extra person still cannot use it without valid credentials.

2. Make the app validate with your backend

On first run, and then periodically, the app should call your server and verify:
	•	this user exists
	•	this account is enabled
	•	this version is allowed
	•	this device is registered or under the device limit

If validation fails, the app should lock.

This is the core. Without a backend check, local login alone is weak because local files/config can often be tampered with.

3. Bind access to a device fingerprint, but don’t trust it too much

You can store a device ID and allow only one or two devices per user.

This helps against casual sharing, but do not fool yourself: device fingerprinting on desktop apps is bypassable by a determined user. It is a speed bump, not a vault.

4. Keep critical logic server-side where possible

This is the real dividing line.

If your app’s value is entirely local, then anyone sufficiently motivated can inspect it, patch it, or rebuild enough of the workflow. If some important functionality depends on your server, copied apps become far less useful because the valuable part is not sitting on their disk.

So ask yourself:
	•	Is the app mostly a local tool?
	•	Or can the important actions depend on an API/backend you control?

If it is fully local, your protection ceiling is much lower.

What I would not rely on by itself

Just having users and login

Not enough by itself.

If the app only checks login locally, someone can often bypass the check.

Hiding the code

That helps only a bit. If you ship a desktop app, people already have the binary.

Trusting “friends”

This is where founders get lazy and stupid. Most leaks are not malicious master hackers. They are careless people forwarding files to “just one mate.”

What else I suggest

Private beta distribution

Use a private distribution channel:
	•	private GitHub repo or private release access
	•	direct download links that you can rotate
	•	invite-only tester list

GitHub documents releases as the standard packaged distribution mechanism, and private repos are treated as confidential. That helps keep access limited at the distribution stage.  ￼

Code signing

Sign the installer/app if you can. This is more about trust and reducing security warnings than anti-copying, but it matters. Microsoft Defender SmartScreen evaluates downloaded apps/files and can warn users about suspicious downloads. Signing improves legitimacy and reduces friction for testers.  ￼

Basic legal layer

Have testers agree in writing:
	•	private beta only
	•	no redistribution
	•	no resale
	•	no reverse engineering
	•	feedback may be used by you

This won’t stop bad actors technically, but it makes expectations explicit and gives you a clean reason to cut access immediately.

Telemetry

Track:
	•	who logs in
	•	device count
	•	IP/country anomalies
	•	repeated failed logins
	•	app version usage

If one tester suddenly appears on five devices, you know exactly who leaked it.

The blunt truth about protection

If your app is:
	•	local-only
	•	built with common desktop tech
	•	distributed as an installer
	•	used by testers on their own computers

then you cannot truly stop copying. You can only raise the cost.

If the app becomes valuable, assume that:
	•	someone will share the installer
	•	someone may inspect the app
	•	someone may try to bypass login
	•	casual testers will be your biggest leak source, not elite pirates

That is reality.

Best approach for your stage

For a friends-only beta, do this:
	1.	Private download link only
	2.	Individual accounts per tester
	3.	Mandatory online login on first launch
	4.	Server-side license validation on launch and every X hours/days
	5.	Max 1–2 devices per account
	6.	Remote disable per user
	7.	Basic terms: no sharing, no resale
	8.	Log usage so leaks are traceable

That is the highest ROI setup.

My recommendation

Yes, implement the user/login approach, but do it properly:
	•	not just “users”
	•	users + backend validation + device limit + remote revocation

That will stop most casual copying, which is probably your real threat right now.

What I would not do is waste weeks chasing military-grade DRM. For a small beta, that is ego-driven overengineering. Your real job is to validate whether the app is useful, while making leaks inconvenient enough.
